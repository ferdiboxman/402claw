# Welcome to the Skill Era

The most valuable companies of the past decade didn't make things. They moved things.

Stripe moved money. Twilio moved messages. Plaid moved data. They built pipes—infrastructure that sat between intention and execution. And they got paid every time something flowed through.

That was the API era. It minted millionaires and billionaires. It was a great game.

It's also over.

---

## The API Era (2010-2024)

From 2010 to 2024, APIs were the ultimate distribution hack. You didn't need to sell to humans. You sold to developers, who built products that sold to humans. Leverage on leverage.

The playbook was simple:
1. Find something complex (payments, identity, messaging)
2. Abstract it into a clean interface
3. Charge per transaction
4. Watch usage compound

Stripe didn't need a sales team knocking on doors. One developer integrated payments over a weekend, and suddenly their whole company was a Stripe customer forever.

This worked because *execution* was the bottleneck. Knowing you needed to send an SMS wasn't hard. Actually sending an SMS at scale? That was hard. APIs solved execution.

But what happens when execution becomes trivial?

---

## The Shift

Large language models don't just generate text. They compress execution.

Think about what an LLM actually does. You give it a prompt—a description of what you want—and it produces output. The gap between "I want X" and "here's X" collapsed from hours or days to seconds.

This changes everything about APIs.

In the old world, you called Twilio to send an SMS because you couldn't build SMS infrastructure yourself. The *capability* was the moat.

In the new world, an AI agent can write code to send SMS, deploy it, and run it. Infrastructure commoditized overnight. Any agent can spin up capability on demand.

So what's left that's actually scarce?

*Judgment.*

The agent can send an SMS. But what should the SMS say? When should it be sent? To whom? With what tone? In what context?

These aren't technical problems. They're expertise problems. And expertise is the new moat.

---

## The Skill Era (2025+)

We're entering an era where *skills*—not APIs—are the atomic unit of value.

A skill is packaged judgment. It's the playbook, the heuristic, the pattern that took you years to develop, encoded in a way that agents can invoke.

- An SEO expert doesn't need to explain SEO to a client anymore. They deploy their audit skill, agents call it, they get paid per invocation.
- A pricing strategist doesn't consult. Their pricing framework runs 10,000 times a day while they sleep.
- A legal risk assessor doesn't review contracts manually. Their judgment scales to every contract that needs review.

This is the inversion: In the API era, you owned pipes and charged for throughput. In the skill era, you own patterns and charge for judgment.

Skills are the new APIs. Expertise is the new infrastructure.

And here's the wild part: the addressable market is *everyone with expertise*. Not just developers. Not just companies with engineering teams. Anyone who has encoded knowledge worth paying for.

The accountant who knows exactly how to structure a small business. The marketer who can write headlines that convert. The recruiter who knows what separates good candidates from great ones.

All of them can now turn that knowledge into a callable, payable, scalable service.

---

## What This Means for Builders

If you're building in 2025, the game has changed.

**Stop building commodity infrastructure.** If an agent can replicate it in 30 seconds, it's not defensible. Execution is a race to zero.

**Start packaging expertise.** The value is in *knowing what to do*, not in *being able to do it*. Your moat is the pattern, not the pipe.

**Think in invocations, not subscriptions.** The agentic internet doesn't want to sign up for your SaaS and manage another subscription. It wants to call you, pay you, and get a result. One transaction. Zero friction.

**Encode your playbooks.** That process you've refined over years? That framework that always works? That checklist you run through? That's your skill. That's what scales.

The builders who win in the skill era won't be the ones who build the best APIs. They'll be the ones who package the best judgment.
